Strix Varia

Barred Owl

In Roman mythology, the Strix was a large foreboding creature that fed on human flesh, associated with witches, and foretold ill omens. It was associated with the appearance and calls of owls. The barred owl certainly can feel a bit offputting or portending of misfortune. Their eyes are black, calls bizarre, and movement silent. While riding my bike along a trail one night, I had one land mere feet away and cause me to nearly fall off in fright. It's probably one of my favorite memories being able to see one up close.

Tyrannus
Verticalis

Western Kingbird

These kingbirds are named as such for their tendency to act like kings and aggressively scare off larger birds like hawks or even people, hence the Tyrannus which is derived from tyrant. They migrate up to Kansas in flocks during the Summer to lay eggs. They can be found higher in trees or sometimes nesting in electric poles. Some people characterize their vocalizations as squeaky toys, personally, I hear it as a "chweep-chuweep" type of sound.

Cardinalis

Northern Cardinal

I'm not exactly sure what the reasoning was for being named cardinalis. It could be because it means chief in Latin and male cardinals have a chieftan-esque look. All I know is these birds whistle a lot and the difference between male and female is stark.

Hirundo
Rustica

Barn Swallow

Hirundo Rustica loosely translates to countryside swallow. Rustica is closer to rustic than rust and isn't referencing the rust colored belly that they have. I really like swallows for their flight patterns and interesting nests. They can often by found on the underside of bridges or overhangs near water.

Zenaida
Macroura

Mourning Dove

They might be called doves, but they're basically pigeons. The name Zenaida comes from the Greek term for "dedicated to zeus", however the doves themselves were named after the wife of the person who first catalogued the species. Zeus' bird is actually an eagle. Macroura means long tail. In Kansas, they can be found year-round pretty much anywhere, just listen for their soft coo-ing sounds. You can also often find mating pairs "kissing" or "billing" where they affectionately peck at eachother's heads.

Ardea
Herodias

Great Blue Heron

The Great Blue Heron's scientific name isn't very interesting as the Ardea portion is just the word for Heron in Latin, and Herodias is the same but for Greek. I actually took most of these pictures just off of campus on the little walking trail in front of the ITC.

Cyanocitta
Cristata

Blue Jay

Cyano means dark blue and kitta(citta) means jay in Greek, while cristata means tufted or crested. So their scientific name is basically crested dark blue jay. Blue jays belong to the corvidae family and are related to crows, ravens, and magpies.
